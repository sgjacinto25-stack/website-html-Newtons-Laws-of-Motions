Newton's Third Law of Motion

Action and Reaction

Newton’s Third Law says that forces always come in pairs. For every action force, there is an equal force in the opposite direction called the reaction force. When object A pushes or pulls object B, object B pushes or pulls object A with the same amount of force but in the opposite direction. These pairs of forces act on different objects and can happen in both contact and non-contact interactions.

Forces always occur in pairs called action and reaction forces, and they happen because of interactions between objects, so a force cannot exist alone. Since these forces act on different objects, they do not cancel each other out.

For example, when a person pushes a wall, the wall pushes back with the same amount of force. The wall does not move because it is strongly supported by the building and the ground.

Understanding Action and Reaction

Action–reaction force pairs, according to Newton’s Third Law, happen at the same time and are equal in size but opposite in direction. They do not cancel each other because they act on different objects (for example, the foot pushes the ground and the ground pushes the foot). These interaction pairs occur in many situations such as walking, swimming, and rocket propulsion.

When you walk, your foot pushes backward on the ground. The ground pushes your foot forward with an equal force, allowing you to move. Without this reaction force from the ground, walking would not be possible.

Real-Life Examples

Example Explanation Picture
Walking When a person walks, their foot pushes backward on the ground. The ground pushes forward with an equal reaction force that moves the person ahead.
Swimming Swimmers push water backward with their arms and legs. The water pushes them forward with an equal force.
Rocket Launch A rocket pushes hot gases downward out of its engine. The gases push the rocket upward with an equal reaction force.

Key Points to Remember

  • Every action force has an equal and opposite reaction force.
  • Action and reaction forces occur at the same time.
  • The forces act on different objects.
  • The forces are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.
